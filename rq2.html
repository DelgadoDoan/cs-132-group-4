RQ2: Relationship with GVA

Correlation tests (Pearson) to explore association between household education spending and GVA (Gross Value Added).

Interpretation

The graph above compares the Household Final Consumption Expenditure (HFCE) in education with the Gross Value Added (GVA) of the Philippines from 2000 to 2025. The two series generally move in tandem, showing a strong positive correlation between educational spending and overall economic growth.

Periods of economic expansion, reflected by increasing GVA, correspond to growth in household spending on education, indicating that households tend to invest more in learning when incomes are higher. Conversely, dips during crises or slowdowns reflect how vulnerable education budgets are to macroeconomic conditions. This can be seen in the year 2020 due to the COVID-19 Pandemic.

This relationship underscores how education expenditure can both drive and respond to economic development, highlighting the interdependence between household welfare and national productivity.

Data Transformation and Normality

Before performing correlation analysis, both variables—HFCE and GVA—were log-transformed to stabilize variance and normalize their distributions. The histograms above display the post-log distributions for each variable.

Log transformation reduces skewness and mitigates the impact of outliers, ensuring that the data better meet the assumptions of the Pearson correlation test. After transformation, both HFCE and GVA approximate a normal distribution, validating the statistical assumptions necessary for accurate correlation analysis.

Correlation Analysis

The scatterplots above illustrate the relationship between HFCE in education and GVA in both their original (left) and log-transformed (right) forms. In both cases, the points form a clear upward trend, indicating a strong positive correlation between the two variables.

The log-transformed plot further emphasizes linearity and homoscedasticity—key assumptions of Pearson’s correlation test. This confirms that as national productivity (GVA) increases, household expenditure on education also rises proportionally, reinforcing the significant association between economic performance and educational investment.

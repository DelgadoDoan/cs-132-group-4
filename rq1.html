RQ1: Spending Patterns

Time-series and comparative trend analysis across education, food, housing, and healthcare (2000-2025).

Distribution of Spending and Economic Output

The first figure illustrates the distributions of Household Final Consumption Expenditure (HFCE) in education and Gross Value Added (GVA). Both distributions are positively skewed, meaning most values cluster at lower spending levels, with fewer years recording higher expenditures.

For HFCE in education, the majority of spending values fall between ₱75,000 million and ₱125,000 million, with a gradual decline in frequency as spending exceeds ₱150,000 million. This indicates that household education spending has historically been concentrated at modest levels but has shown consistent growth in later years.

The GVA distribution follows a similar pattern, with most data points between ₱100,000 million and ₱150,000 million. The tail toward higher values (up to ₱275,000 million) suggests substantial economic expansion in the education sector and related industries over time.

Together, these distributions imply that while education expenditure and value added have steadily grown, the increases are uneven , with significant acceleration occurring only in the most recent decade.

Category-Level Time Series Analysis

The second figure presents quarterly HFCE data from 2000 to 2025 across key consumption categories. The long-term trend reflects consistent economic growth, increasing household spending capacity, and evolving consumption priorities over time.

The category Food and non-alcoholic beverages remains the dominant spending group, rising from approximately ₱450 billion in 2000 to over ₱1.6 trillion by 2025. This steady climb captures both population growth and inflationary effects, maintaining food as the foundation of Filipino household budgets.

Education follows a more moderate but stable trajectory, increasing from around ₱70 billion in 2000 to nearly ₱230 billion by 2025. Notably, slight dips around 2013 (Typhoon Yolanda) and 2020 (COVID-19 pandemic) reflect temporary spending disruptions before recovery resumed, showing the sector's resilience.

Health expenditures also exhibit strong upward growth, climbing from under ₱100 billion in 2000 to around ₱300 billion by 2025, underscoring increasing household attention to medical needs, preventive care, and aging demographics.

Other essential sectors, such as housing, water, and electricity, display consistent yet moderate increases, aligning with urbanization trends and infrastructure expansion.

Key Findings for RQ1

[RQ1] How have household expenditures on education, relative to other major household spending categories, changed from 2000 to 2025?

  • Education expenditure rose significantly from approximately ₱70 billion in 2000 to nearly ₱250 billion by 2025, an increase of more than 250%. This growth outpaced that of most other household spending categories, indicating a long-term shift toward prioritizing human capital and educational attainment.
  • While food and housing remain the largest components of total household consumption, their proportional share has steadily declined. In contrast, education, health, and communication expenditures have grown faster, reflecting an expanding service-oriented consumption pattern among Filipino households.
  • Periods of economic instability, such as Typhoon Yolanda in 2013 and the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, led to short-term declines in education spending, but these were followed by strong rebounds by 2022-2023. This indicates that households view education as an essential and resilient expenditure, even during crises.

Overall: From 2000 to 2025, household spending patterns in the Philippines reveal a clear evolution toward education-driven and welfare-oriented consumption. Education has transformed from a secondary expenditure into a major and steadily growing category, suggesting that Filipino households increasingly value long-term social mobility and human capital investment over purely subsistence-based spending.
